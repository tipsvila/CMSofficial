2. The Reality: Operational Costs

While you don't pay to get the contract, once you win one, you have to do the work. This is where "capital" comes into play:

Payroll & Labor: If you hire people to help fulfill the contract, you must pay them.

Materials & Supplies: If your subcontract requires you to supply parts or equipment, you will typically need to purchase those materials before you are paid by the Prime Contractor.  
Mobilization Funding

Cash Flow (The "Float"): Government and large corporate contracts often operate on "Net 30," "Net 60," or even "Net 90" payment terms. This means you do the work today, but you might not get paid for 30–90 days. You need enough cash to cover your expenses during that waiting period.
